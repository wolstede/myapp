Linxem is a word game for 2-4 players playing in Play and Pass mode, where all players use the same mobile device and hand it round to each other as required. It can also be played in practice mode by one player.

Overview

The game involves a series of challenges directly involving a pair of the players, who are, in essence, working together to win the challenge.

In a challenge, one player of the pair, the setter, is shown a set of display words (actually words or names) with different backgrounds. Some of these backgrounds are and, normally, some are , although there is an option to play with only ones. The backgrounds are important.

The setter must look at these display words and select 2 or more of them that are linked together in some way. Generally, display words with a background are selected, as those with ones lead to penalties.

Having selected these, the setter must then input or say out loud a Linxem. A Linxem is a word, name, title, occupation, number or date that links them (>> "links 'em", >> "Linxem") in some way.

The other player of the pair, the solver, is then shown the full set of display words, rearranged and with no distinctive backgrounds, and must then try to identify the display words selected by the setter from the Linxem given by the setter.

Points are won for correct identification of the display words selected, but are lost for identifying display words whose original backgrounds were . Points are also lost for failing to complete the setting or solving within the specified countdown time or for providing an invalid Linxem.

Linxem is a fairly unusual game in that it is primarily concerned with cooperation, rather than competition. Its scoring system introduces an element of competition, but the primary enjoyment of the game comes from being part of or observing the cooperative challenges.

Hints and enhancements

Particularly when playing the two-player game as a pastime, where there is no real competition, it can be more interesting and enjoyable to select a risky, more obscure Linxem rather than a more obvious one.

The fun of the game can be greatly increased if solvers convey their thoughts out loud.

What is a Linxem?

A Linxem connects the selected display words in some way. It might:
  • be a word that encompasses the selected display words, e.g. 'Mammals' for the words 'Cat' and 'Dog';
  • be something that is associated with them, e.g. 'America' for the words 'Washington' and 'Texas';
  • have two different meanings and be related to the selected display words individually, e.g. 'Twist' for 'Dance' and 'Dickens';
  • be something that could go before or after the selected display words, e.g. 'Cream' for 'Ice' and 'Hand';
  • describe the word structure in some way, e.g. 'Monosyllabic' for 'Ice' and 'Hand', or 'Reversals' for 'God' and 'Dog', or even 'Rhyming' for 'White' and 'Right';
  • be applicable to a homophone of a selected display word, e.g. 'Fish' for the words 'Place' and 'Perch';
  • refer to the sounds of the words only, e.g. 'Roman numerals' for 'Ivy' and 'Sea'.
Overall, as long as the Linxem is valid as defined below, it is acceptable. Of course, whether your partner in the challenge will get it is a different matter.

It is acceptable to say the Linxem in a way that suggests some unusual meaning, e.g. with an upward inflection at the end to make it sound questionable, or to add a question mark if typing it.

Valid and invalid Linxems

A valid Linxem is a word, a hyphenated word or a compound word found in an English dictionary. So, the following would all normally be considered valid (even though unlikely in the Linxem game):
  • Coal
  • Coalman
  • Coal-fired
  • Coal tar
  • Turn in
  • Turn-on
  • Turnover
  • Well-to-do
Note: expressions such as 'French cheeses', 'Animals native to Australia' or 'Words containing 10 letters' would not be acceptable as they wouldn't be found in a dictionary.

A Linxem may also be an occupation, e.g. 'Tennis player', a number, e.g. '2012', an address, e.g. '221B Baker Street', or a date/time, e.g. '23 April 1616', 'July 4th' or '10:50'.

Additionally, a Linxem may be a recognized name or title (fact or fiction), e.g. of a person, animal, team, event, building, brand, place, book, play, band, song, album, series, TV programme, painting, art work, or movie. So, the following would also normally be considered valid Linxems:
  • Nat King Cole
  • Old King Cole
  • Charles Dickens
  • Miss Havisham
  • A Tale of Two Cities
  • Tennessee Williams
  • Verdi
  • Giuseppe Verdi
  • La Traviata
  • Seabiscuit
  • The Empire State Building
  • London
  • The Thinker
  • The Great Escape
  • Daft Punk
  • Get Lucky
  • Saudi Arabia
  • New Zealand
  • Forty-niners
  • The Queen of England
  • Princess Diana
  • U.S. President
  • Hillary Clinton
Recongnized abbreviations, acronyms and initials, such as FBI, IBM, BP, BBC or ASAP are also acceptable as Linxems.

The name/title should be reasonably well-known and can be subject to checking. Stating the name 'French cheeses' or 'Australian animals' in the hope (or maybe even knowledge) that there might be an obscure book with such a name (and there probably is), as a way of giving an otherwise invalid Linxem, is not allowed.

There are, of course, words or names that can't be used with certain selected words. The principle is simple: for a given selected display word and Linxem, one may not include the other, either in spelling or in sound, and may not be directly derived from or be an inflection of the same root word.

So, if one of the words were 'Go', the other could not be:
  • Goes
  • Gone
  • Went
  • Forgo
  • Forwent
  • Golly (includes the spelling of Go)
  • Gauche (includes the sound of Go)
Likewise, if one of the words were 'Grass', the other could not be 'Graze' or 'Grazing'.

If either of the two contains more than one word, this applies to the individual words also, so, if the selected display word were 'New York', the following would not be valid Linxems:
  • New Yorker
  • New England
  • New
  • York
  • Yorkshire
  • Yorker
Spoken vs typed Linxems

The setter can choose whether to say the Linxem out loud or type it into the input field. It is, of course, generally easier just to say it, but remember that a spoken Linxem will not be remembered by the system if you exit the game and come back later. There are times, however, when one form of Linxem will be preferred by the setter over the other. For example, if linking the words 'Hurricane' and 'Clock', a typed-in Linxem 'Wind' would provide such a link, whereas saying either of the two pronunciations would not. In contrast, when trying to link 'Fish' and 'London', the spoken Linxem 'Plaice' or 'Place' would be better than either of these words typed.

Points won and lost

All points won go to the pair involved in the challenge, while points lost in the challenge go to the opposition.

The pair gain points for each display word identified by the solver that the setter had selected;

The pair gain a bonus of points if the setter has selected 3 or more display words and the solver gets them all correct;

The pair lose points for each display word selected by the solver that originally had a background;

The pair lose points if the setter fails to select at least two display words before the countdown ends;

The pair lose points if the Linxem is successfully challenged and rejected as being invalid;

The pair lose points if the solver fails to select the exact number of display words selected by the setter before the countdown ends.

Clearly, given this scoring system, the setter would normally select display words with a background, since these do not incur a penalty, and would be wary of selecting any with a background. However, the setter may sometimes choose to select display words with a background. Consider the following scenarios:
  • the setter cannot see a link between even two of the display words with a background. Failure to select two would result in the loss of points. However, selecting one display word with a background as well as one with a background could result in a gain of points if the solver gets them both correct, balanced by a loss of points because one had a background.
  • the setter can see a link between two display words with a background and one with a background. Although there would be a loss of points because one had a background, assuming the solver identified them correctly, this would be more than compensated for by the points for the three correct selections plus the bonus of points for getting all three correct.
At the end of the game, i.e. when the last challenge in the series has been completed, the winner is the individual or team (see next) with the highest score.

2, 3 or 4 players or practice mode

The standard game is for four players playing as two teams of two players. In this game, players in a given team alternate in being the setter and solver, and any points won during the challenge go to their team, while the other team receives the points lost.

The game may also be played by just two players, as a pastime, in a similar way, where points lost go to a non-existent team called Anon. The objective is to gain more points than you lose to the opposition or, of course, just to have fun setting and solving the challenges regardless of the scores.

The game may also by played by 3 or 4 players trying to win as individuals. In these games, players come together to play as pairs in a challenge. Any points won in the challenge are shared equally by the two players in the pair, while the opposition takes the points lost during the challenge; in the case of 4 players, the points lost are shared equally by the two other players. All players act as setter and solver in challenges with every other player. The winner is the individual with the highest total at the end of the game.

The game may also by played by one player in practice mode, as a pastime. The setter tries to select linkable display words and mentally choose a Linxem, but there is no solving phase: selections made by the setter are assumed to be successfully selected by the non-existent solver. Points lost through selecting display words with a yellow background or through failing to select at least two display words before the countdown ends go to a non-existent team called Anon., but the real objective is, of course, to practise setting challenges, so the scores shown are simply indicative of how well you might have done in a real game with a good solver.

Green-only variant

The game may also be played using backgrounds that are only green for the setter, i.e. with no yellow backgrounds. This means that points cannot be lost by the solver for choosing words that originally had a yellow background. Clearly, this is a simpler game. It may be appropriate when playing with those new to the game, or when playing as a pastime rather than playing competitively.

Playing the game

A game is started by tapping the New button. You then have to choose the game you wish to play and tap the Play button to the right of the choice.

The next screen shown explains the mode of play, including, importantly for a game for two pairs, who is to play with whom as partners. N.B. players 1 and 3 play together against 2 and 4 in a game for two pairs, as it is best for partners in a team to sit opposite each other around a table so that the opposition players can both see the device easily. Initials are used to refer to the players (initially set to PA, PB, PC and PD). You may choose to change the initials used by tapping the Change button. Note: in the unlikely event that there are duplicates in the initials, the only option on this screen is to change the initials.

Underneath the Change button, you can select or deselect the option to play the variant with green backgrounds only. Tap on the small empty box to select the option, which turns the box black. Tapping again deselects the option, and turns it to empty again.

The screen also lets you define the length of the countdown used. Simply tap the countdown time you wish to select, which then takes on a green background. The countdown is used both in the setting and solving phases. The default countdown time is 3 minutes - probably a good countdown time to start with.

Once the initials and countdown time are set appropriately, you generally, except in the game for 4 individual players, have a choice of game length, specified by the number of challenges involved. In all these choices, except 1-player practice, every player has the same number of turns at being setter and solver.

Playing a challenge

The system chooses who is to be the setter (and the solver in 3- or 4-player individual games), and advises that the device should be passed to the setter. The setter should take the device and tap OK to reveal the main screen.

The main screen displays display words: ones, and ones. The countdown starts and the setter must select, by tapping, 2 or more of them that can be linked. Clearly, the main focus should be on the ones, as encouraging the solver to select ones will lead to penalties.

The initials of the setter are shown under the Word Word Word button. The number of display words selected by the setter is shown on the right (below the Word Word button).

Points to note about selecting display words in this phase and in the solving phase are:
  • the background becomes a deeper shade and the text becomes white to show that it has been selected;
  • tapping the word again deselects it, if you wish to change your selection.
Once this is complete, the setter may tap an OK button. An error message is displayed if fewer than 2 display words have been selected, and the game continues.

Note that the background of the countdown display changes to orange when it is within 15 seconds of stopping. If the countdown stops before the OK button is tapped, play continues as if it had been tapped, unless fewer than two display words have been selected, in which case the challenge ends and the pair involved in the challenge lose points to the opposition.

Advice is then given that the setter should input (through the device keyboard) or say the Linxem out loud (one or the other, not both). The Linxem is input through the field with the light red background (it was silver earlier) with the word Linxem displayed lightly as a prompt. In all games apart from the two-player version, other players (apart from the solver) should then be consulted about the choice of Linxem. If valid objections are raised about a Linxem that has been typed in, the setter should be given an opportunity to modify it. If valid objections are raised about a Linxem that has been said out loud, the Reject button should be tapped. This ends the challenge and the opposition receive points. If the Linxem is acceptable (and all players should be generous in this regard), the OK button should be tapped, which then brings up a screen advising that the device should be handed to the solver.

Note that all players except the solver may see the device during the setting phase, and all players may see it during the solving phase. This keeps everyone involved and able to see how cunning the choice of Linxem is and how the solver seems to be doing.

The device should then be handed to the solver, who is informed by the system how many display words need to be selected and should click OK to see the main screen. The display words are redisplayed, but re-ordered from the setting phase and all with a background. The countdown starts and the solver must tap on the exact number of display words that appear to best match the stated Linxem. Note: during the countdown the solver may select more than the number of display words selected by the setter to help decision-making, but the number must be exactly equal to the number selected by the setter when the countdown ends or when the OK button is tapped to end the countdown early. If the numbers do not match when the OK button is tapped, an error message is displayed and the game continues. If the numbers do not match when the countdown reaches zero, the pair involved in the challenge lose points to the opposition. This loss is displayed, together with the running totals, and an OK button is displayed which should then be tapped to enable the solver to see the selections made by the setter.

During this phase and for the rest of the challenge, the solver's initials are displayed on the left under the Word Word Word button, and the number of display words to be selected is displayed on the right.

Throughout the challenge, the challenge number is shown with a blue background to the left of the countdown, while the total number of challenges in the game is shown to the right of the countdown, also with a blue background.

Reviewing and Scoring

Once the solving phase is over, and the correct number of selections has been made, the display words selected by the solver are shown with the background and text colour that they had at the end of the setting phase. The solver can easily see which ones are correct (those with a deep background and white text) and which are incorrect (those with a light background and black text).

The setter should tap a Score button when ready. The points gained by the pair in the challenge and lost to the opposition are displayed, together with the new running totals for the game. Tapping OK takes you on to the next challenge, assuming there are still some to go, or, if the selections made did not all correspond to those made by the setter, the selections made by the setter are first displayed to the solver, with the backgrounds and text colour that they had at the end of the setting phase.

Starting a new game

The New button may be tapped at any time to start a new game. If a game is already underway and not over, you will be asked to confirm that you wish to start a new game.

Continuing a game

If you exit the app once a game is underway, the game state is stored. The game continues when you open the app again.

Layout of display words

The standard layout of the display words is in 3 columns. If you are using a small device, you may wish to see them with a larger text size. You are able to do so by tapping the Word Word button to the right of the countdown field, which lays them out in 2 columns with larger text size. You may change back to 3 columns by tapping the Word Word Word button to the left of the countdown field. The layout button for the current layout option is displayed with a white background, the other with a silver background.

These buttons also change the text size of the Linxem field and the fields on either side of this, so that they match the text size of the display words.
