Introduction by Mr. Charles Pooter

\r\n

Why should I not publish my diary?  I have often seen \r\nreminiscences of people I have never even heard of, and I fail to \r\nsee—because I do not happen to be a ‘Somebody’—why my diary \r\nshould not be interesting.  My only regret is that I did not \r\ncommence it when I was a youth.

\r\n

Charles Pooter.

Chapter I

\r\n

We settle down in our new home, and I resolve to keep a diary.  Tradesmen trouble us a bit, so does the scraper.  The Curate calls and pays me a great compliment.

\r\n

My dear wife Carrie and I have just been a week in our new house, “The \r\nLaurels,” Brickfield Terrace, Holloway—a nice six-roomed residence, not counting \r\nbasement, with a front breakfast-parlour.  We have a little front garden; \r\nand there is a flight of ten steps up to the front door, which, by-the-by, we \r\nkeep locked with the chain up.  Cummings, Gowing, and our other intimate \r\nfriends always come to the little side entrance, which saves the servant the \r\ntrouble of going up to the front door, thereby taking her from her work.  \r\nWe have a nice little back garden which runs down to the railway.  We were \r\nrather afraid of the noise of the trains at first, but the landlord said we \r\nshould not notice them after a bit, and took £2 off the rent.  He was \r\ncertainly right; and beyond the cracking of the garden wall at the bottom, we \r\nhave suffered no inconvenience.

\r\n


“The Laurels”

\r\n

After my work in the City, I like to be at home.  What’s the good of a \r\nhome, if you are never in it?  “Home, Sweet Home,” that’s my motto.  I \r\nam always in of an evening.  Our old friend Gowing may drop in without \r\nceremony; so may Cummings, who lives opposite.  My dear wife Caroline and I \r\nare pleased to see them, if they like to drop in on us.  But Carrie and I \r\ncan manage to pass our evenings together without friends.  There is always \r\nsomething to be done: a tin-tack here, a Venetian blind to put straight, a fan \r\nto nail up, or part of a carpet to nail down—all of which I can do with my pipe \r\nin my mouth; while Carrie is not above putting a button on a shirt, mending a \r\npillow-case, or practising the “Sylvia Gavotte” on our new cottage piano (on the \r\nthree years’ system), manufactured by W. Bilkson (in small letters), from \r\nCollard and Collard (in very large letters).  It is also a great comfort to \r\nus to know that our boy Willie is getting on so well in the Bank at \r\nOldham.  We should like to see more of him.  Now for my diary:—

\r\n\r\n<NP>\r\n

April 3

Tradesmen called for custom, and I promised \r\nFarmerson, the ironmonger, to give him a turn if I wanted any nails or \r\ntools.  By-the-by, that reminds me there is no key to our bedroom door, and \r\nthe bells must be seen to.  The parlour bell is broken, and the front door \r\nrings up in the servant’s bedroom, which is ridiculous.  Dear friend Gowing \r\ndropped in, but wouldn’t stay, saying there was an infernal smell of paint.

\r\n


Our dear friend Gowing.

\r\n\r\n<NP>\r\n

April 4

Tradesmen still calling; Carrie being \r\nout, I arranged to deal with Horwin, who seemed a civil butcher with a nice \r\nclean shop.  Ordered a shoulder of mutton for to-morrow, to give him a \r\ntrial.  Carrie arranged with Borset, the butterman, and ordered a pound of \r\nfresh butter, and a pound and a half of salt ditto for kitchen, and a shilling’s \r\nworth of eggs.  In the evening, Cummings unexpectedly dropped in to show me \r\na meerschaum pipe he had won in a raffle in the City, and told me to handle it \r\ncarefully, as it would spoil the colouring if the hand was moist.  He said \r\nhe wouldn’t stay, as he didn’t care much for the smell of the paint, and fell \r\nover the scraper as he went out.  Must get the scraper removed, or else I \r\nshall get into a scrape.  I don’t often make jokes.

\r\n


Our dear friend Cummings.

\r\n\r\n<NP>\r\n

April 5

Two shoulders of mutton arrived, Carrie \r\nhaving arranged with another butcher without consulting me.  Gowing called, \r\nand fell over scraper coming in.  Must get that scraper removed.

\r\n<NP>\r\n

April 6

Eggs for breakfast simply shocking; sent \r\nthem back to Borset with my compliments, and he needn’t call any more for \r\norders.  Couldn’t find umbrella, and though it was pouring with rain, had \r\nto go without it.  Sarah said Mr. Gowing must have took it by mistake last \r\nnight, as there was a stick in the ‘all that didn’t belong to nobody.  In \r\nthe evening, hearing someone talking in a loud voice to the servant in the \r\ndownstairs hall, I went out to see who it was, and was surprised to find it was \r\nBorset, the butterman, who was both drunk and offensive.  Borset, on seeing \r\nme, said he would be hanged if he would ever serve City clerks any more—the game \r\nwasn’t worth the candle.  I restrained my feelings, and quietly remarked \r\nthat I thought it was possible for a city clerk to be a \r\ngentleman.  He replied he was very glad to hear it, and wanted to \r\nknow whether I had ever come across one, for he hadn’t.  He left the \r\nhouse, slamming the door after him, which nearly broke the fanlight; and I heard \r\nhim fall over the scraper, which made me feel glad I hadn’t removed it.  \r\nWhen he had gone, I thought of a splendid answer I ought to have given \r\nhim.  However, I will keep it for another occasion.

\r\n<NP>\r\n

April 7

Being Saturday, I looked forward to being \r\nhome early, and putting a few things straight; but two of our principals at the \r\noffice were absent through illness, and I did not get home till seven.  \r\nFound Borset waiting.  He had been three times during the day to apologise \r\nfor his conduct last night.  He said he was unable to take his Bank Holiday \r\nlast Monday, and took it last night instead.  He begged me to accept his \r\napology, and a pound of fresh butter.  He seems, after all, a decent sort \r\nof fellow; so I gave him an order for some fresh eggs, with a request that on \r\nthis occasion they should be fresh.  I am afraid we shall have to \r\nget some new stair-carpets after all; our old ones are not quite wide enough to \r\nmeet the paint on either side.  Carrie suggests that we might ourselves \r\nbroaden the paint.  I will see if we can match the colour (dark chocolate) \r\non Monday.

\r\n<NP>\r\n

April 8
Sunday

After Church, the Curate came back \r\nwith us.  I sent Carrie in to open the front door, which we do not use except \r\non special occasions.  She could not get it open, and after all my display, \r\nI had to take the Curate (whose name, by-the-by, I did not catch,) round the \r\nside entrance.  He caught his foot in the scraper, and tore the bottom of \r\nhis trousers.  Most annoying, as Carrie could not well offer to repair them \r\non a Sunday.  After dinner, went to sleep.  Took a walk round the \r\ngarden, and discovered a beautiful spot for sowing mustard-and-cress and \r\nradishes.  Went to Church again in the evening: walked back with the \r\nCurate.  Carrie noticed he had got on the same pair of trousers, only \r\nrepaired.  He wants me to take round the plate, which I think a great \r\ncompliment.

\r\n<NP>\r\n

Chapter II

\r\n

Tradesmen and the scraper still troublesome.  Gowing rather tiresome with his complaints of the paint.  I make one of the best jokes of my life.  Delights of Gardening.  Mr. Stillbrook, Gowing, Cummings, and I have a little misunderstanding.  Sarah makes me look a fool before Cummings.

\r\n<NP>\r\n

April 9

Commenced the morning badly.  The \r\nbutcher, whom we decided not to arrange with, called and blackguarded me \r\nin the most uncalled-for manner.  He began by abusing me, and saying he did \r\nnot want my custom.  I simply said: “Then what are you making all this fuss \r\nabout it for?”  And he shouted out at the top of his voice, so that all the \r\nneighbours could hear: “Pah! go along.  Ugh!  I could buy up ‘things’ \r\nlike you by the dozen!”

\r\n

I shut the door, and was giving Carrie to understand that this disgraceful \r\nscene was entirely her fault, when there was a violent kicking at the door, \r\nenough to break the panels.  It was the blackguard butcher again, who said \r\nhe had cut his foot over the scraper, and would immediately bring an action \r\nagainst me.  Called at Farmerson’s, the ironmonger, on my way to town, and \r\ngave him the job of moving the scraper and repairing the bells, thinking it \r\nscarcely worth while to trouble the landlord with such a trifling matter.

\r\n

Arrived home tired and worried.  Mr. Putley, a painter and decorator, \r\nwho had sent in a card, said he could not match the colour on the stairs, as it \r\ncontained Indian carmine.  He said he spent half-a-day calling at \r\nwarehouses to see if he could get it.  He suggested he should entirely \r\nrepaint the stairs.  It would cost very little more; if he tried to match \r\nit, he could only make a bad job of it.  It would be more satisfactory to \r\nhim and to us to have the work done properly.  I consented, but felt I had \r\nbeen talked over.  Planted some mustard-and-cress and radishes, and went to \r\nbed at nine.

\r\n<NP>\r\n

April 10

Farmerson came round to attend to the \r\nscraper himself.  He seems a very civil fellow.  He says he does not \r\nusually conduct such small jobs personally, but for me he would do so.  I \r\nthanked him, and went to town.  It is disgraceful how late some of the \r\nyoung clerks are at arriving.  I told three of them that if Mr. Perkupp, \r\nthe principal, heard of it, they might be discharged.

\r\n

Pitt, a monkey of seventeen, who has only been with us six weeks, told me “to \r\nkeep my hair on!”  I informed him I had had the honour of being in the firm \r\ntwenty years, to which he insolently replied that I “looked it.”  I gave \r\nhim an indignant look, and said: “I demand from you some respect, sir.”  He \r\nreplied: “All right, go on demanding.”  I would not argue with him any \r\nfurther.  You cannot argue with people like that.  In the evening \r\nGowing called, and repeated his complaint about the smell of paint.  Gowing \r\nis sometimes very tedious with his remarks, and not always cautious; and Carrie \r\nonce very properly reminded him that she was present.

\r\n<NP>\r\n

April 11

Mustard-and-cress and radishes not come up \r\nyet.  To-day was a day of annoyances.  I missed the quarter-to-nine \r\n’bus to the City, through having words with the grocer’s boy, who for the second \r\ntime had the impertinence to bring his basket to the hall-door, and had left the \r\nmarks of his dirty boots on the fresh-cleaned door-steps.  He said he had \r\nknocked at the side door with his knuckles for a quarter of an hour.  I \r\nknew Sarah, our servant, could not hear this, as she was upstairs doing the \r\nbedrooms, so asked the boy why he did not ring the bell?  He replied that \r\nhe did pull the bell, but the handle came off in his hand.

\r\n

I was half-an-hour late at the office, a thing that has never happened to me \r\nbefore.  There has recently been much irregularity in the attendance of the \r\nclerks, and Mr. Perkupp, our principal, unfortunately choose this very morning \r\nto pounce down upon us early.  Someone had given the tip to the \r\nothers.  The result was that I was the only one late of the lot.  \r\nBuckling, one of the senior clerks, was a brick, and I was saved by his \r\nintervention.  As I passed by Pitt’s desk, I heard him remark to his \r\nneighbour: “How disgracefully late some of the head clerks arrive!”  This \r\nwas, of course, meant for me.  I treated the observation with silence, \r\nsimply giving him a look, which unfortunately had the effect of making both of \r\nthe clerks laugh.  Thought afterwards it would have been more dignified if \r\nI had pretended not to have heard him at all.  Cummings called in the \r\nevening, and we played dominoes.

\r\n<NP>\r\n

April 12

Mustard-and-cress and radishes not come up \r\nyet.  Left Farmerson repairing the scraper, but when I came home found \r\nthree men working.  I asked the meaning of it, and Farmerson said that in \r\nmaking a fresh hole he had penetrated the gas-pipe.  He said it was a most \r\nridiculous place to put the gas-pipe, and the man who did it evidently knew \r\nnothing about his business.  I felt his excuse was no consolation for the \r\nexpense I shall be put to.

\r\n

In the evening, after tea, Gowing dropped in, and we had a smoke together in \r\nthe breakfast-parlour.  Carrie joined us later, but did not stay long, \r\nsaying the smoke was too much for her.  It was also rather too much for me, \r\nfor Gowing had given me what he called a green cigar, one that his friend \r\nShoemach had just brought over from America.  The cigar didn’t look green, \r\nbut I fancy I must have done so; for when I had smoked a little more than half I \r\nwas obliged to retire on the pretext of telling Sarah to bring in the \r\nglasses.

\r\n

I took a walk round the garden three or four times, feeling the need of fresh \r\nair.  On returning Gowing noticed I was not smoking: offered me another \r\ncigar, which I politely declined.  Gowing began his usual sniffing, so, \r\nanticipating him, I said: “You’re not going to complain of the smell of paint \r\nagain?”  He said: “No, not this time; but I’ll tell you what, I distinctly \r\nsmell dry rot.”  I don’t often make jokes, but I replied: “You’re talking a \r\nlot of dry rot yourself.”  I could not help roaring at this, and \r\nCarrie said her sides quite ached with laughter.  I never was so immensely \r\ntickled by anything I have ever said before.  I actually woke up twice \r\nduring the night, and laughed till the bed shook.

\r\n<NP>\r\n

April 13

An extraordinary coincidence: Carrie had \r\ncalled in a woman to make some chintz covers for our drawing-room chairs and \r\nsofa to prevent the sun fading the green rep of the furniture.  I saw the \r\nwoman, and recognised her as a woman who used to work years ago for my old aunt \r\nat Clapham.  It only shows how small the world is.

\r\n<NP>\r\n

April 14

Spent the whole of the afternoon in the \r\ngarden, having this morning picked up at a bookstall for fivepence a capital \r\nlittle book, in good condition, on Gardening.  I procured and sowed \r\nsome half-hardy annuals in what I fancy will be a warm, sunny border.  I \r\nthought of a joke, and called out Carrie.  Carrie came out rather testy, I \r\nthought.  I said: “I have just discovered we have got a \r\nlodging-house.”  She replied: “How do you mean?”  I said: “Look at the \r\nboarders.”  Carrie said: “Is that all you wanted me for?”  I \r\nsaid: “Any other time you would have laughed at my little pleasantry.”  \r\nCarrie said: “Certainly—at any other time, but not when I am busy in the \r\nhouse.”  The stairs looked very nice.  Gowing called, and said the \r\nstairs looked all right, but it made the banisters look all wrong, \r\nand suggested a coat of paint on them also, which Carrie quite agreed \r\nwith.  I walked round to Putley, and fortunately he was out, so I had a \r\ngood excuse to let the banisters slide.  By-the-by, that is rather \r\nfunny.

\r\n<NP>\r\n

April 15
Sunday

At three o’clock Cummings and \r\nGowing called for a good long walk over Hampstead and Finchley, and brought with \r\nthem a friend named Stillbrook.  We walked and chatted together, except \r\nStillbrook, who was always a few yards behind us staring at the ground and \r\ncutting at the grass with his stick.

\r\n


Stillbrook lags behind. Going up hill.

\r\n\r\n

As it was getting on for five, we four held a consultation, and Gowing \r\nsuggested that we should make for “The Cow and Hedge” and get some tea.  \r\nStillbrook said: “A brandy-and-soda was good enough for him.”  I reminded \r\nthem that all public-houses were closed till six o’clock.  Stillbrook said, \r\n“That’s all right—bona-fide travellers.”

\r\n


Going down hill.

\r\n\r\n

We arrived; and as I was trying to pass, the man in charge of the gate said: \r\n“Where from?”  I replied: “Holloway.”  He immediately put up his arm, \r\nand declined to let me pass.  I turned back for a moment, when I saw \r\nStillbrook, closely followed by Cummings and Gowing, make for the \r\nentrance.  I watched them, and thought I would have a good laugh at their \r\nexpense, I heard the porter say: “Where from?”  When, to my surprise, in \r\nfact disgust, Stillbrook replied: “Blackheath,” and the three were immediately \r\nadmitted.

\r\n


Nearly there.

\r\n\r\n

Gowing called to me across the gate, and said: “We shan’t be a minute.”  \r\nI waited for them the best part of an hour.  When they appeared they were \r\nall in most excellent spirits, and the only one who made an effort to apologise \r\nwas Mr. Stillbrook, who said to me: “It was very rough on you to be kept \r\nwaiting, but we had another spin for S. and B.’s.”  I walked home in \r\nsilence; I couldn’t speak to them.  I felt very dull all the evening, but \r\ndeemed it advisable not to say anything to Carrie about the matter.

\r\n<NP>\r\n

April 16

After business, set to work in the \r\ngarden.  When it got dark I wrote to Cummings and Gowing (who neither \r\ncalled, for a wonder; perhaps they were ashamed of themselves) about yesterday’s \r\nadventure at “The Cow and Hedge.”  Afterwards made up my mind not to write \r\nyet.

\r\n<NP>\r\n

April 17

Thought I would write a kind little note \r\nto Gowing and Cummings about last Sunday, and warning them against Mr. \r\nStillbrook.  Afterwards, thinking the matter over, tore up the letters and \r\ndetermined not to write at all, but to speak quietly to \r\nthem.  Dumfounded at receiving a sharp letter from Cummings, saying that \r\nboth he and Gowing had been waiting for an explanation of my (mind you, \r\nmy) extraordinary conduct coming home on Sunday.  \r\nAt last I wrote: “I thought I was the aggrieved party; but as I freely forgive \r\nyou, you—feeling yourself aggrieved—should bestow forgiveness on me.”  I \r\nhave copied this verbatim in the diary, because I think it is one of the \r\nmost perfect and thoughtful sentences I have ever written.  I posted the \r\nletter, but in my own heart I felt I was actually apologising for having been \r\ninsulted.

\r\n<NP>\r\n

April 18

Am in for a cold.  Spent the whole \r\nday at the office sneezing.  In the evening, the cold being intolerable, \r\nsent Sarah out for a bottle of Kinahan.  Fell asleep in the arm-chair, and \r\nwoke with the shivers.  Was startled by a loud knock at the front \r\ndoor.  Carrie awfully flurried.  Sarah still out, so went up, opened \r\nthe door, and found it was only Cummings.  Remembered the grocer’s boy had \r\nagain broken the side-bell.  Cummings squeezed my hand, and said: “I’ve \r\njust seen Gowing.  All right.  Say no more about it.”  There is \r\nno doubt they are both under the impression I have apologised.

\r\n

While playing dominoes with Cummings in the parlour, he said: “By-the-by, do \r\nyou want any wine or spirits?  My cousin Merton has just set up in the \r\ntrade, and has a splendid whisky, four years in bottle, at thirty-eight \r\nshillings.  It is worth your while laying down a few dozen of it.”  I \r\ntold him my cellars, which were very small, were full up.  To my horror, at \r\nthat very moment, Sarah entered the room, and putting a bottle of whisky, \r\nwrapped in a dirty piece of newspaper, on the table in front of us, said: \r\n“Please, sir, the grocer says he ain’t got no more Kinahan, but you’ll find this \r\nvery good at two-and-six, with twopence returned on the bottle; and, please, did \r\nyou want any more sherry? as he has some at one-and-three, as dry as a nut!”

Chapter III

\r\n

A conversation with Mr. Merton on Society.  Mr. and Mrs. James, of Sutton, come up.  A miserable evening at the Tank Theatre.  Experiments with enamel paint.  I make another good joke; but Gowing and Cummings are unnecessarily offended.  I paint the bath red, with unexpected result.

\r\n<NP>\r\n

April 19

Cummings called, bringing with him his \r\nfriend Merton, who is in the wine trade.  Gowing also called.  Mr. \r\nMerton made himself at home at once, and Carrie and I were both struck with him \r\nimmediately, and thoroughly approved of his sentiments.

\r\n

He leaned back in his chair and said: “You must take me as I am;” and I \r\nreplied: “Yes—and you must take us as we are.  We’re homely people, we are \r\nnot swells.”

\r\n

He answered: “No, I can see that,” and Gowing roared with laughter; but \r\nMerton in a most gentlemanly manner said to Gowing: “I don’t think you quite \r\nunderstand me.  I intended to convey that our charming host and hostess \r\nwere superior to the follies of fashion, and preferred leading a simple and \r\nwholesome life to gadding about to twopenny-halfpenny tea-drinking afternoons, \r\nand living above their incomes.”

\r\n

I was immensely pleased with these sensible remarks of Merton’s, and \r\nconcluded that subject by saying: “No, candidly, Mr. Merton, we don’t go into \r\nSociety, because we do not care for it; and what with the expense of cabs here \r\nand cabs there, and white gloves and white ties, etc., it doesn’t seem worth the \r\nmoney.”

\r\n

Merton said in reference to friends: “My motto is ‘Few and True;’ and, \r\nby the way, I also apply that to wine, ‘Little and Good.’”  Gowing said: \r\n“Yes, and sometimes ‘cheap and tasty,’ eh, old man?”  Merton, still \r\ncontinuing, said he should treat me as a friend, and put me down for a dozen of \r\nhis “Lockanbar” whisky, and as I was an old friend of Gowing, I should have it \r\nfor 36s., which was considerably under what he paid for it.

\r\n

He booked his own order, and further said that at any time I wanted any \r\npasses for the theatre I was to let him know, as his name stood good for any \r\ntheatre in London.

\r\n<NP>\r\n

April 20

Carrie reminded me that as her old school \r\nfriend, Annie Fullers (now Mrs. James), and her husband had come up from Sutton \r\nfor a few days, it would look kind to take them to the theatre, and would I drop \r\na line to Mr. Merton asking him for passes for four, either for the Italian \r\nOpera, Haymarket, Savoy, or Lyceum.  I wrote Merton to that effect.

\r\n<NP>\r\n

April 21

Got a reply from Merton, saying he was \r\nvery busy, and just at present couldn’t manage passes for the Italian Opera, \r\nHaymarket, Savoy, or Lyceum, but the best thing going on in London was the \r\nBrown Bushes, at the Tank Theatre, Islington, and enclosed seats for \r\nfour; also bill for whisky.

\r\n<NP>\r\n

April 23

Mr. and Mrs. James (Miss Fullers that was) \r\ncame to meat tea, and we left directly after for the Tank Theatre.  We got \r\na ’bus that took us to King’s Cross, and then changed into one that took us to \r\nthe “Angel.”  Mr. James each time insisted on paying for all, saying that I \r\nhad paid for the tickets and that was quite enough.

\r\n

We arrived at theatre, where, curiously enough, all our ’bus-load except an \r\nold woman with a basket seemed to be going in.  I walked ahead and \r\npresented the tickets.  The man looked at them, and called out: “Mr. \r\nWillowly! do you know anything about these?” holding up my tickets.  The \r\ngentleman called to came up and examined my tickets, and said: “Who gave you \r\nthese?”  I said, rather indignantly: “Mr. Merton, of course.”  He \r\nsaid: “Merton?  Who’s he?”  I answered, rather sharply: “You ought to \r\nknow, his name’s good at any theatre in London.”  He replied: “Oh! is \r\nit?  Well, it ain’t no good here.  These tickets, which are not dated, \r\nwere issued under Mr. Swinstead’s management, which has since changed \r\nhands.”  While I was having some very unpleasant words with the man, James, \r\nwho had gone upstairs with the ladies, called out: “Come on!”  I went up \r\nafter them, and a very civil attendant said: “This way, please, box H.”  I \r\nsaid to James: “Why, how on earth did you manage it?” and to my horror he \r\nreplied: “Why, paid for it of course.”

\r\n

This was humiliating enough, and I could scarcely follow the play, but I was \r\ndoomed to still further humiliation.  I was leaning out of the box, when my \r\ntie—a little black bow which fastened on to the stud by means of a new \r\npatent—fell into the pit below.  A clumsy man not noticing it, had his foot \r\non it for ever so long before he discovered it.  He then picked it up and \r\neventually flung it under the next seat in disgust.  What with the box \r\nincident and the tie, I felt quite miserable.  Mr. James, of Sutton, was \r\nvery good.  He said: “Don’t worry—no one will notice it with your \r\nbeard.  That is the only advantage of growing one that I can see.”  \r\nThere was no occasion for that remark, for Carrie is very proud of my beard.

\r\n

To hide the absence of the tie I had to keep my chin down the rest of the \r\nevening, which caused a pain at the back of my neck.

\r\n<NP>\r\n

April 24
